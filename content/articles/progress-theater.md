**Progress theater** is the organizational pattern where talking about work substitutes for doing it — where the discussion becomes the deliverable and meetings serve as evidence of effort rather than vehicles for it. The term was coined by Goran Trajkovski in a 2026 Substack essay synthesizing organizational psychology research on why this pattern is so durable.

The core observation: in a meeting, raising a concern, asking a question, or suggesting a direction all register as participation. They're visible the entire time they're happening. Actual work — fixing the data pipeline, writing the document, closing the ticket — is invisible until it ships. When both are counted equally as contribution, rational actors bias toward talking.

## Why it persists

### Incentive design

Bolino (1999) found that people routinely engage in behaviors that signal effort rather than produce outcomes. The system doesn't distinguish between contribution that moves work forward and contribution that fills airtime. A comment in a meeting evaporates. A shipped product stays — and carries your name.

So when talking and doing are both counted as participation, and talking is lower-risk, talking wins.

### Social loafing

Karau and Williams (1993) ran a meta-analysis of 78 studies showing that people exert less effort when working collectively than individually. The effect is strongest when individual contributions are hard to identify and when the group outcome doesn't obviously depend on any one person — which describes exactly the kind of meeting where nobody knows why they're there and there's no clear deliverable.

### Alignment as stalling

Anderson (2003) studied decision avoidance and found that when uncertainty is high and stakes feel visible, people delay action by expanding analysis. In organizational settings this shows up as: "We need more alignment before we move forward." Requesting more input postpones the moment of commitment. The information won't improve the outcome. It just delays accountability.

### Psychological safety without ownership

Edmondson's (1999) foundational work on psychological safety was about learning behavior — people who feel safe take bigger risks and admit mistakes. Somewhere the application drifted. In many organizations, psychological safety became about getting everyone to speak with nobody clearly responsible to act. When it's safe to speak but unclear who owns what, conversation expands and action stalls.

## The research numbers

Cross, Rebele, and Grant (2016) surveyed more than 300 organizations and found that time spent in collaborative activities had increased by 50% or more over two decades. At many companies, people now spend roughly 80% of their time in meetings, on calls, or responding to colleague requests — leaving 20% for the work those conversations are supposedly about.

The distribution is also lopsided: 20–35% of value-added collaboration comes from just 3–5% of employees. The people doing most of the talking aren't doing most of the work.

Asana's State of Work Innovation report (2024) measured the downstream cost: individual contributors waste 3.7 hours per week in unproductive meetings (up 118% from 2019), and managers waste 5.8 hours. 84% of employees who experience "meeting hangovers" spend additional time venting to colleagues afterward. One unproductive meeting generates three layers of waste: lost time, recovery venting, and cognitive overhead before real work can resume.

## Three-stage progression

Trajkovski offers a hierarchy:

**Talking that leads to doing** — a decision is made, someone walks out with a clear task. This is what meetings are for.

**Talking that replaces doing** — no decision is made, but everyone feels like something happened. The conversation becomes the deliverable. Less visible than inaction, which is why it persists.

**Talking about talking** — meetings about meetings. Syncs to prepare for syncs. The calendar is full and full calendars feel productive.

The second stage is the trap. The third is where it leads if nobody intervenes.

## The diagnostic question

One question matters more than any other at the end of a meeting: *what is different now.*

If the answer is nothing, the meeting was theater. The fix isn't fewer meetings — it's making doing as visible as talking. Track the ratio of meeting hours to actual outputs. If you manage a team, notice what your culture rewards: if raising a concern in a meeting is valued the same as resolving one outside of it, you've built a system that produces talk.
