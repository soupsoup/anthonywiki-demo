**Permeable organizations** is a framework for thinking about cross-functional capability building. The central reframe: organizational silos are not a failure of leadership or culture. They are a natural byproduct of organizational scaling — teams develop their own language, priorities, and definitions of success. The goal isn't to destroy them. It's to make them permeable.

## The core argument

Silos form because specialization works. Teams that develop deep domain expertise — security, data, cloud infrastructure — outperform generalist teams on domain-specific problems. The dysfunction isn't the silo; it's the impermeability. When knowledge can't cross boundaries, you get:

- "Whose problem is this?" meetings
- Slow cross-functional handoffs
- Repeated escalations for problems adjacent-domain knowledge would have prevented
- Longer mean time to resolution on incidents that span teams

The permeable organization model accepts silos as permanent features and designs around them, rather than running futile flattening initiatives.

## How innovation happens at intersections

The practical upside of permeability isn't just efficiency — it's that innovation happens at the boundaries between different perspectives. An InfoSec team and a Data team solving the same problem from different angles will generate better solutions than either team working in isolation. What feels like a water cooler conversation is actually cross-pollination. Permeable organizations formalize it.

## Making silos permeable in practice

**Visibility first.** Give teams insight into what other teams are actually working on — not org chart abstractions but real work in progress. Understanding what the data team is wrestling with changes how the security team thinks about a shared problem.

**Shared problem-solving projects.** Create work that requires two or three teams to solve together. Passive learning (a cloud engineer sitting in on security meetings) builds contextual awareness. Active collaboration builds vocabulary and empathy.

**Vocabulary and empathy as the actual output.** The measurable outcome of cross-skilling isn't completion rates on training modules — it's whether people can talk across domain boundaries under pressure. That's a vocabulary problem and an empathy problem, not a knowledge problem.

## Measuring it right

Traditional training metrics fail for cross-skilling. Course completion rates don't equal capability. Hours of training don't equal competence. You can finish a cloud architecture course and still make poor decisions under pressure.

Better indicators:
1. Faster cross-functional handoffs
2. Fewer escalated issues caused by adjacent-domain knowledge gaps
3. Reduced mean time to resolution
4. Fewer "whose problem is this?" meetings

## Leadership failures that kill permeability

- **Performative commitment**: Announcing a learning culture without changing how the operating rhythm recognizes learning behaviors
- **"Rules for them, not for me"**: Leaders who don't visibly learn signal that learning is for contributors, not for leadership
- **No recognition for learning**: If you don't reward people for building adjacent knowledge, they won't do it

The example from the session that works: a CIO who shares one thing they're learning in every team update. It normalizes the behavior, and it surfaces people with hidden breadth who were suppressing it due to impostor syndrome.

## Individual Development Plans

The framework suggested: three technology paths relevant to the current role plus one or two that the individual wants to explore. The "wants to explore" element creates agency rather than obligation — learning feels "for them," not "done to them." Thirty percent of people who leave roles cite lack of organizational investment in their development.
